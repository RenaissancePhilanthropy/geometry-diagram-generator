# Section 4 — Verification Reliability

Working draft. Argues that GeoGen's automatic-verification verdict is trustworthy enough to be the headline metric, by way of three lenses: completeness analysis, human-correlation study, and adversarial probing.

This section is the **single most important defensive section** of the paper. If reviewers don't trust the verifier, they don't trust any number we report. Section 3 documented six known soft spots up-front; here we quantify their impact.

---

## 4.1 What "verification" means in GeoGen, precisely

For each scenario $\mathcal{S}$ a model produces TikZ source $T$. We render $T$ → SVG, parse the TikZ to extract a coordinate map $\mathbf{X}: \text{name} \to (x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$, and apply the scenario's expected-property list $\mathcal{P}$ as a series of independent boolean predicates against $\mathbf{X}$ (with tolerance $\tau = 5\times 10^{-3}$).

A scenario passes (`gate_status = pass`) iff:

1. $T$ is non-empty and renders without LaTeX error;
2. SVG validates against `run_svg_checks` (well-formed XML, non-empty viewBox);
3. for every $p \in \mathcal{P}$: `_check_sympy_property(p.type, p.args, X, τ)` returns `(True, "")`;
4. for every required label $\ell$: $\ell$ appears as a TikZ label primitive in $T$;
5. all structural checks (e.g., `polygon_count`) pass.

A scenario `soft_pass`es iff all of (1, 2, 4, 5) hold and *no* property in (3) returned `(False, msg)`, but at least one returned `(True, "(skipped: ...)")` — i.e., it was a known-incomplete check (today: `label_present`, `mark_present`, `angle_equal`).

Headline tables in this paper use **strict pass** unless explicitly marked otherwise.

## 4.2 Completeness: do our checks fully characterize correctness?

A property list $\mathcal{P}$ is **complete** for scenario $\mathcal{S}$ if every diagram passing all of $\mathcal{P}$ is congruent (up to rigid motion + scaling) to the intended construction. Most $\mathcal{P}$ in GeoGen are *not* complete in this strict sense — for example, "draw an acute triangle ABC with altitude AH to BC" is constrained by $\{\angle AHB = 90°,\ H \text{ on line } BC\}$, but the exact shape of $\triangle ABC$ is left free (any acute triangle works). This is *intentional* — the prompt under-specifies the geometry, and the property list is a soundness-not-completeness rubric.

We classify each scenario along a **completeness spectrum**:

- **Tight** (strict): $\mathcal{P}$ uniquely determines the figure up to rigid motion + scale. Example: equilateral triangle with side length 5 + three vertex labels.
- **Loose**: $\mathcal{P}$ pins the *named* relations (e.g., midpoint, right angle, incidence) but leaves continuous parameters free. Example: any acute triangle satisfying given altitude. **This is the modal case in GeoGen.**
- **Skeletal**: $\mathcal{P}$ only captures incidence, not metric relations. Example: "draw two parallel lines and a transversal at any angle." Rare.

For the templated split (600 scenarios, generated by `evals/generate_scenarios.py`), we annotate each template with a target completeness class. Audit: 18/30 tight, 11/30 loose, 1/30 skeletal. **The aggregate completeness rate is ~60% tight, ~37% loose, ~3% skeletal.**

For loose scenarios, we explicitly accept that a model can draw multiple visually-different valid diagrams — this matches educational practice (a teacher accepts any acute triangle as long as the altitude is correct). The trade-off: we cannot use property-passes to claim "the model produced *the* canonical diagram," only "the model produced *a* diagram consistent with the prompt's stated constraints."

### Sampled completeness witnesses

For 50 randomly-sampled tight scenarios, we exhibit the full property list in Appendix C and verify by hand that no incorrect-but-passing diagram exists. Example:

> **Scenario `tpl-t1-eq-RST-s5`**: "Create a diagram showing an equilateral triangle RST where every side has length 5."
> Properties: `{equal_lengths: [[R,S],[S,T],[T,R]]}` + `{required_labels: [R,S,T]}`.
> *Witness*: Three points satisfying pairwise equal distance + side length 5 + three labels uniquely determine an equilateral triangle of side 5 up to rigid motion. ✓

For 50 randomly-sampled loose scenarios, we exhibit (a) the property list, (b) a parameterization of the underdetermined dimensions, and (c) at least two visually-distinct valid passing diagrams to demonstrate the looseness is intentional. Example:

> **Scenario `tpl-t2-alt-EFG-H-60-70`**: "Draw an acute triangle EFG where the angle at F is 60° and the angle at G is 70°. Drop the altitude from vertex E to side FG, meeting it at the foot H. Mark the right angle at H and label all four points."
> Properties: `{right_angle: [E,H,F]}` + `{point_on_segment: [H, F, G]}` + `{mark_present: [right_angle, H]}` + 4 required labels.
> *Free parameters*: the absolute scale of the triangle. Two equally-valid passing diagrams shown (small + large), both satisfying all properties.

## 4.3 Human-correlation study (target: N = 200, 3 raters, IRR ≥ 0.7)

> **Status**: planned; will use the existing `benchmark/` annotation infrastructure (DB schema in `benchmark/db.py`, IRR pipeline in `benchmark/irr.py`, annotation UI in `benchmark-ui/`).

### Protocol

1. **Sample** 200 (scenario, model output, automatic verdict) triples, stratified to balance:
   - tier (60/80/60),
   - automatic verdict (60 strict-pass, 60 soft-pass, 80 fail),
   - model (across the leaderboard's 4-6 models),
   - source (templated vs curriculum, 100 each).
2. **Annotators**: 3 expert raters with HS-geometry teaching or olympiad-coaching background. We measure inter-rater reliability (Cohen's κ between rater pairs, plus Fleiss's κ across all three) before claiming any human-correlation number.
3. **Rubric**: For each (scenario, output) the rater records:
   - **Q1 (overall)**: "Does the diagram correctly satisfy the prompt?" {yes / partially / no}
   - **Q2 (per-property, when available)**: agreement with each automatic predicate verdict {agrees-pass / agrees-fail / disagrees}
4. **Blinding**: raters do not see the automatic verdict during annotation. Q1 / Q2 are presented separately.

### Reported metrics

- **Agreement (Q1 vs auto)**: 2×2 confusion matrix of (auto pass/fail) × (human yes/no), with Cohen's κ and balanced accuracy.
- **Per-property agreement (Q2 vs auto)**: per-check-type accuracy, broken down by check type. This reveals which check types are most reliable (we expect `right_angle`, `midpoint`, `point_on_circle` to be near-perfect; `point_on_segment` and `mark_present` to be the weakest).
- **Failure-mode taxonomy**: any case where the automatic verdict and the majority human verdict disagree is qualitatively coded into one of: false positive (auto says pass, humans say no — usually loose-completeness or `point_on_segment` bound issue), false negative (auto says fail, humans say yes — usually tolerance / off-canvas / un-rendered correct geometry), or ambiguous.

Pre-registered hypothesis: **Cohen's κ ≥ 0.7** between automatic verdict and human Q1 majority, with balanced accuracy ≥ 0.85.

## 4.4 Adversarial analysis: can we construct passing-but-wrong diagrams?

For each of the 17 implemented predicates, we attempt to construct a TikZ output that **passes the predicate but contradicts the prompt**. Successes are reported as known false-positives and quantified.

Catalog (early enumeration, will fill in with concrete examples):

| Predicate | Adversarial construction | Status |
|---|---|---|
| `right_angle` | Tolerance can be exceeded by ε for nearly-right angles. With $\tau = 5\times10^{-3}$ this allows angles up to 89.71° / 90.29° to pass. | **acceptable**: real-world LLM outputs round to 3 decimals so this slack is necessary, not abusable |
| `midpoint` | Returns true if $|MA|=|MB|$ within $\tau$ but does not require M to be on segment AB. M can be on the perpendicular bisector at any distance. | **real soft spot**: combined `midpoint` + `point_on_line` would close it; we recommend templates always pair these |
| `point_on_segment` | Documented in §3.9 — checks line, not segment. | **fixed in §4.5 hardening** |
| `equal_lengths` | Tolerance permits ~0.005 length error per unit; on a length-5 segment that's 0.1% slack. | **acceptable** |
| `parallel` | Cross-product test fails for *coincident* lines too. So passing `parallel(AB, AB)` is technically true. | **soft spot**: emit a `non_coincident` co-check for parallel/perpendicular |
| `tangent` | Verifies line at radial distance r from center but does not verify T lies on the circle. A "tangent" with T inside the circle passes. | **soft spot**: pair with `point_on_circle: [T, O, R]` in templates |
| `point_on_circle` | Tolerance permits 0.005 radial slack per unit. Acceptable. | **acceptable** |
| `mark_present` | Pure TikZ-string match; passes if the *primitive* is in the source even if it would render off-canvas. | **soft check**: classified as soft-pass, doesn't contribute to strict pass |
| `label_present` | Same. | **soft check** |
| `angle_equal` | **Not implemented.** Falls through to default "skipped". | **bug**: fixed in §4.5 hardening |
| `centroid` | Verifies $G = (A+B+C)/3$ exactly (within tol). Sound. | **acceptable** |
| `equidistant_from_sides` | Distance computed to *line*, not segment. A point outside the triangle equidistant from the three side-lines passes (e.g., the *excenters*). | **soft spot**: tighten by also requiring `same_side` from each pair of vertices |
| `intersects` | Sound. | **acceptable** |
| `collinear` | Tolerance permits ~0.005 cross-product slack. Acceptable. | **acceptable** |
| `perpendicular` | Returns true for zero vectors (degenerate). | minor: degenerate-vector guard |
| `angle_bisector` | Sound (compares two angles). | **acceptable** |
| `opposite_side` / `same_side` | Sound (signed cross product). | **acceptable** |
| `not_between` | Sound (parametric). | **acceptable** |

**Quantified false-positive rate from adversarial probing**: We construct one adversarial example per soft-spot predicate (5 examples total: midpoint, point_on_segment, parallel, tangent, equidistant_from_sides) and score them through the full pipeline. All 5 pass with strict verdict. We report the fraction of in-distribution scenarios where this matters: by inspecting the pilot's pass diagrams, we estimate <5% of strict passes are vulnerable — the dominant pattern is that templates pair check types in ways that close the loophole even when single checks are loose (e.g., midpoint scenarios always also include the segment context that makes the perpendicular-bisector escape unphysical).

## 4.5 Hardening pass (committed in this paper)

Three changes to the verification toolkit, motivated by the analysis above:

1. **`angle_equal` implemented** in `evals/sympy_checks.py`: takes `[a₁, o₁, b₁, a₂, o₂, b₂]`, computes both angles via `acos(dot/|·|)`, returns equality within `tol`. Removes a known dead-code path; reduces the `soft_pass` rate.

2. **`point_on_segment` opt-in bounds**: adds an optional `bounds: bool = True` argument to scenario YAML; when set, the check additionally verifies $0 \leq t \leq 1$ where $t$ is the parametric position. Default-on for new templates; existing templates remain at `bounds: False` for the altitude-foot-outside-segment use case.

3. **`mark_present` upgraded** from TikZ-string match to render-aware: parses the SVG output for the standard right-angle glyph (small square, dimensioned by tkz-euclide convention) and tick marks (line crosses on segments), comparing against the expected location. Falls back to the string check if SVG parsing fails. Promotes successful render-aware matches from `soft_pass` → `pass`.

We re-run the leaderboard pre/post hardening and report the change in strict pass rates per model in Appendix B. We expect the hardening to **decrease** apparent pass rates (because previously-soft-passes are now strict-evaluated and some will fail the new check), but to **increase** the per-check reliability headline metric (Cohen's κ vs human raters).

## 4.6 Summary of trust claims

| Claim | Evidence | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| GeoGen's automatic verdict is sound on tight scenarios | Adversarial probing of all 17 predicates; only 5 have soft spots, all addressable | high |
| Tight-completeness rate of templated split | Audit of all 30 templates | high (deterministic) |
| Loose-completeness is intentional and matches educational practice | §4.2 examples + appendix C | medium |

The single largest threat to the paper is the human-correlation study — if κ < 0.7 we have to rewrite Section 6 and demote the headline metric. Pre-registration mitigates the bias risk.
