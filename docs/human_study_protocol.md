# GeoGenBench human-correlation study — protocol

**Authored:** 2026-05-02 (frozen before annotation begins; see git history of this file for revisions).
**Annotation window:** 2026-05-02 evening through 2026-05-05 inclusive.
**Status:** pre-registered prior to data collection.

This document is the canonical pre-registration for the human-correlation study reported in `paper/sections/04_verification_reliability.tex` (`§ssec:human`) and `paper/appendices/E_human_study.tex`.
It is committed before annotation begins; any deviation from this protocol is logged at the bottom of this file and surfaced in the appendix.

---

## 1. Research question

Does the GeoGenBench automatic verifier's verdict (`pass` / `soft_pass` / `fail`) on a (prompt, rendered-diagram) pair agree with the consensus judgment of two domain-expert humans?

We pre-register a single primary endpoint:

- **Cohen's $\kappa$ (auto-verdict, human-consensus) $\geq 0.7$** on the binary collapse `pass-or-soft-pass` vs `fail`, computed across the full N=200 sample.

Secondary endpoints (not gating, reported regardless):

- Cohen's $\kappa$ on the three-way verdict (`pass` / `soft_pass` / `fail`).
- Inter-rater reliability between the two human raters (Cohen's $\kappa$ on the two-way collapse before consensus discussion).
- Per-property Q2 agreement rates, broken down by the 17 implemented predicates.
- Balanced accuracy of the auto-verdict against human consensus on the binary collapse.

## 2. Raters

Two raters, both authors of the paper, anonymized for review.
Credentials reported in the appendix:

- Rater A: PhD in mathematics; co-author of the paper.
- Rater B: award-winning K–12 mathematics educator with middle-school teaching experience; co-author of the paper.

Conflict-of-interest disclosure: both raters are project authors, which is a real but standard limitation for benchmark papers; we mitigate via blinding (§5) and by reporting per-rater independent agreement before consensus.
We discuss this limitation explicitly in `§ssec:human` and the discussion section.

## 3. Sample

- **Source:** `evals/results/leaderboard_pilot_v3/*.jsonl` — the canonical post-hardening pilot, 498 records.
- **Sampling pool:** 400 records — all 498 minus 98 records with no rendered SVG (all 98 are `fail` due to unparseable model output; nothing visual to rate).
- **Target N:** 200 records.
- **Stratification (deterministic, `random.seed(42)`):**
  1. Take all 28 records with `gate_status="soft_pass"` (verifier was uncertain — the most epistemically informative stratum).
  2. Take all 35 records with `gate_status="fail"` and a rendered SVG (the entire pool of "rendered-but-failed" cases).
  3. Sample the remaining **137 records** from `gate_status="pass"`, balancing across tier × strategy × model.
     For cells with fewer records than the per-cell budget, take all available and redistribute the deficit to the most populous cells.
- **Source split:** all 200 are templated (the pilot does not include curriculum runs; we acknowledge this as a stratum-coverage limitation in the appendix, and queue a curriculum follow-up for a future paper).
- **Model coverage:** all six models present in the pilot will be represented in the sample. Equal per-model balance is not feasible because two models have small SVG-rendered populations (opus N=42, gemini-flash N=22 with rendered SVGs); the sampler will take everything available from those and balance the remainder.

The exact sample is generated by `evals/human_study/sample_human_study.py` and frozen as `evals/human_study/sample.json`; both are committed to the repo.
**Re-running the sampler with the same seed produces a byte-identical sample.**

## 4. Rubric

For each (prompt, diagram, auto-verdict) triple, the rater records:

**Q1 — Overall verdict (single-select, mandatory):**

> Does this diagram correctly satisfy every claim in the prompt?
>
> - **Yes** — every prompt clause is geometrically satisfied.
> - **Partial** — some clauses are satisfied, some are violated.
> - **No** — at least one major prompt clause is violated, or the diagram is unrelated to the prompt.

The "Partial" option exists to capture genuine ambiguity; the binary collapse used for the primary endpoint maps `Partial → No` (conservative).

**Q2 — Per-property agreement (one row per implemented predicate, mandatory):**

For each predicate the auto-verifier checked on this diagram, the rater records:

> Looking at the diagram, do you agree with the verifier's verdict on `{predicate_name}`?
>
> - **Agrees-pass** — verifier said pass and the property holds.
> - **Agrees-fail** — verifier said fail and the property does not hold.
> - **Disagrees** — verifier said the opposite of what is true.
> - **Unsure** — diagram is ambiguous on this property.

Q2 is per-predicate, not per-prompt; a typical diagram has 2–4 predicate rows.
The full predicate set under check is in `paper/appendices/B_property_reference.tex`.

**Free-text notes (optional):** A single text field per item for raters to record disagreement-coding notes ("verifier missed an obtuse-angle assumption," etc.). These feed the qualitative coding section of the appendix.

## 5. Blinding

**Q1 is collected blind to the auto-verdict and to the verifier's per-predicate verdicts.**
The rating tool (single-page HTML viewer) shows: the prompt, the rendered SVG, and the rubric — and **does not** show the auto-verdict or any predicate values until Q1 is submitted.

After Q1 is submitted for an item, Q2 unlocks and shows the auto-verdict's per-predicate values; the rater then judges agreement against each one.
Q2 is **not blind** — agreement is the construct of interest, not independent re-derivation.

The rater's identity is recorded in the response file (one CSV per rater) but is not revealed to the other rater during independent annotation.

## 6. Workflow and consensus

1. **Independent annotation.** Each rater works through all 200 items independently. No discussion during this phase.
2. **Disagreement extraction.** A script (`evals/human_study/compute_kappa.py`) reads both response CSVs, computes inter-rater $\kappa$ (Q1 binary, Q1 three-way, Q2 per-predicate), and writes a list of disagreement items.
3. **Consensus discussion.** The two raters meet (synchronously, ≤ 90 minutes) and discuss each disagreement item. The output is a single consensus label per item per question.
   Consensus is by discussion to convergence; if a single item cannot be resolved within 5 minutes, it is recorded as "irreconcilable" and excluded from the primary endpoint (with the count reported transparently).
4. **Final analysis.** The same script re-runs against the consensus labels to produce the headline κ(auto, consensus) and the confusion matrices reported in the appendix.

## 7. Pre-registered hypothesis (primary endpoint)

> $\kappa(\text{auto-verdict}, \text{human-consensus}) \geq 0.70$ on the binary collapse (`pass + soft_pass` vs `fail`), N = 200, computed across the full sample.

A 95% confidence interval for $\kappa$ is reported via the standard asymptotic formula (Fleiss, Cohen, & Everitt 1969); we additionally report a bootstrap CI (1000 resamples) as a robustness check.

If the lower bound of the 95% CI exceeds 0.70, we report the verifier as **validated against expert human judgment** at the standard interpretive threshold ("substantial agreement," Landis & Koch 1977).

If the point estimate is between 0.40 and 0.70 (or the CI straddles 0.70), we report the result as **moderate agreement, with disagreement coded qualitatively** in the appendix.

If the point estimate is below 0.40, we report the result as **failure to validate** and revise the relevant headline claims in the paper.

## 8. Pre-specified secondary analyses

To be reported regardless of the primary endpoint, in App E:

- **Per-tier subgroup**: κ(auto, consensus) computed separately on T1, T2, T3.
- **Per-strategy subgroup**: κ separately on `raw_code`, `structured`, `recipe`.
- **Per-model subgroup**: κ per vendor (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google) — at the model-tier the per-cell N is too small for vendor-internal subgroup κs.
- **Q2 per-predicate**: agreement rate broken down by the 17 implemented predicates.
- **Disagreement coding**: the consensus-discussion notes are coded by category (e.g., "verifier's tolerance was too loose," "verifier missed an implicit prompt constraint," "rater error caught in consensus") and counts reported.

## 9. Anti-p-hacking commitments

- The primary endpoint and statistical procedure are frozen by this document before any rating begins.
- Sample selection is deterministic and seeded; the exact sample is in version control (`evals/human_study/sample.json`).
- We do **not** add or remove items from the sample after annotation begins.
- We do **not** add or change the primary endpoint after seeing any data.
- All secondary analyses are listed in §8; any additional post-hoc analysis is flagged as such in the appendix.

## 11. Limitations (acknowledged before data collection)

- **Two raters, not three.** Standard inter-rater statistics for $\geq 3$ raters (Fleiss's $\kappa$, intraclass correlation) are not applicable; we report Cohen's pairwise κ.
- **Raters are paper authors.** Conflict of interest is real; we mitigate by Q1 blinding and by reporting per-rater independent agreement before consensus.
- **Templated split only.** Curriculum-extracted prompts are not represented in the sample because the pilot did not include them. We queue this for follow-up.
- **Three-day data collection window.** Annotation is not exhaustive deliberation; a typical item receives 2–4 minutes of rater attention.
