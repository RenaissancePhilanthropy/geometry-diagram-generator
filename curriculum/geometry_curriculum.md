## Volume 1

### Module 1: Reasoning with Shapes

#### Topic 1: Geometry Reasoning

> Students investigate the foundational building blocks of geometry—undefined terms (point, line, plane), defined terms (ray, line segment, angle), and formal reasoning structures including conditional statements, postulates, theorems, conjectures, and inductive/deductive reasoning.

**Lesson 0: Introduction to the Problem-Solving Model and Learning Resources**

*What resources are available in this course to help you extend your mathematical thinking?*

- **Activity 0.GS: Getting Started: You Already Know A Lot!** — Students describe different strategies they use to learn new skills, identify their motivation to learn, and compare strategies with classmates to establish collaboration as a learning approach.
  - *diagram: none | concepts: learning strategies, collaboration, self-reflection*
- **Activity 1: Learning Resources** — Students use the problem-solving model (Notice and Wonder, Organize and Mathematize, Predict and Analyze, Test and Interpret, Report), the TEKS mathematical process standards, and the Academic Glossary to solve a real-world distance problem involving the Pythagorean Theorem on a coordinate grid.
  - *diagram: coordinate grid with two points and a right triangle | concepts: Pythagorean Theorem, distance on a coordinate plane, problem-solving model, right triangle*
- **Activity 0.TT: Talk the Talk: The Problem-Solving Model** — Students reflect on and summarize each phase of the problem-solving model (Notice and Wonder, Organize and Mathematize, Predict and Analyze, Test and Interpret, Report).
  - *diagram: none | concepts: problem-solving model, mathematical reasoning, self-reflection*

**Lesson 1: Points, Lines, Planes, Rays, and Line Segments**

*You have studied points, lines, and planes. Why are these concepts foundational to the study of Geometry?*

Key terms: point  ·  line  ·  collinear points  ·  plane  ·  compass  ·  straightedge  ·  sketch  ·  draw  ·  construct  ·  coplanar lines  ·  non-collinear points  ·  skew lines  ·  ray  ·  endpoint of a ray  ·  line segment  ·  endpoints of a line segment  ·  congruent line segments

- **Activity 1.GS: Getting Started: Points and Lines** — Students investigate the undefined terms point and line. They analyze a diagram with multiple labeled points and lines, practice naming lines using two points or a lowercase letter, and explore collinear points. They examine student reasoning about unique line names through single points versus two points.
  - *diagram: multiple labeled points and lines on a plane | concepts: point, line, collinear points, naming conventions for lines, undefined terms*
- **Activity 1.1: Planes** — Students investigate the third undefined term, plane. They examine five figures showing various intersections of three planes, list all possible ways three planes can intersect, sketch intersections of a line and a plane, and identify coplanar and skew lines in three-dimensional contexts.
  - *diagram: three-dimensional plane intersection diagrams and line-plane intersection sketches | concepts: plane, non-collinear points, intersection of planes, coplanar lines, skew lines, sketch vs. draw vs. construct, compass, straightedge*
- **Activity 1.2: Using Undefined Terms to Define New Terms** — Students use undefined terms to define ray and line segment. They sketch and label rays, distinguish ray AB from ray BA, draw and measure line segments, use rulers to measure segment length, interpret equality versus congruence notation, identify congruent line segments using tick marks, classify triangles based on congruence markers, and write valid congruence and equality statements.
  - *diagram: rays, line segments, triangles with congruence markers | concepts: ray, endpoint of a ray, line segment, endpoints of a line segment, congruent line segments, congruence symbol, equality vs. congruence, isosceles triangle, equilateral triangle, symbolic notation*
- **Activity 1.TT: Talk the Talk: Making a Valid Statement** — Students are given a diagram and name all points, lines, and planes shown. They explain when to use equality versus congruence statements. They then name all lines, rays, and segments shown in a second figure containing two labeled points.
  - *diagram: labeled geometric diagram with points, lines, and a plane; second diagram with two collinear labeled points | concepts: naming points, lines, and planes, symbolic notation for lines, rays, and segments, congruence vs. equality statements, undefined terms vs. defined terms*

**Lesson 2: Formal Reasoning in Euclidean Geometry**

*How can you reason more formally in Euclidean geometry with postulates and theorems?*

Key terms: conditional statement  ·  counterexample  ·  hypothesis  ·  conclusion  ·  postulate  ·  theorem  ·  validity  ·  Euclidean geometry  ·  proof  ·  great circle  ·  spherical geometry

- **Activity 2.GS: Getting Started: Counterexamples** — Students explore two reasons why a conclusion may be false—assumed information is false, or the argument is invalid. They write examples of false conclusions for each reason and provide counterexamples to demonstrate that general statements (including one about prime numbers and one about acute angle sums) are not true.
  - *diagram: none | concepts: counterexample, valid argument, invalid argument, hypothesis, conclusion, false conclusion*
- **Activity 2.1: Conditional Statements** — Students are introduced to conditional statements in if-then form (p → q), identifying hypothesis and conclusion. They analyze all four combinations of true/false p and q for a geometric conditional statement about a 32° angle being acute, then repeat the analysis for a congruence conditional statement. They also evaluate a conditional about segment midpoints and consider a counterexample involving non-collinear points.
  - *diagram: diagram showing non-collinear points with two congruent segments to a common vertex | concepts: conditional statement, hypothesis, conclusion, validity, if-then statement, p implies q, counterexample to conditional, congruent line segments*
- **Activity 2.2: Postulates and Theorems** — Students are introduced to postulates and theorems as building blocks of Euclidean geometry. They study Euclid's five postulates and five common notions from The Elements. They are then introduced to non-Euclidean geometry and spherical geometry, examining great circles as spherical lines, comparing parallel lines and triangle angle sums between Euclidean and spherical geometry, and reasoning about properties of spherical triangles.
  - *diagram: sphere with great circles, spherical triangle with three right angles, parallel line diagrams in Euclidean vs. hyperbolic vs. elliptic geometry | concepts: postulate, theorem, proof, Euclidean geometry, non-Euclidean geometry, spherical geometry, great circle, parallel lines on a sphere, sum of interior angles of a spherical triangle, Euclid's postulates*
- **Activity 2.TT: Talk the Talk: While You Were Away…** — Students write a short note to a peer explaining conditional statements and validity, including definitions of hypothesis and conclusion, examples of all four combinations of true/false p and q, and their own original conditional statement with analysis.
  - *diagram: none | concepts: conditional statement, hypothesis, conclusion, validity, if-then form, mathematical communication*

**Lesson 3: Making Conjectures and Deductive Reasoning**

*How can you apply formal geometric reasoning to what you know?*

Key terms: inductive reasoning  ·  deductive reasoning  ·  conjecture  ·  diagonal  ·  angle  ·  vertex of an angle  ·  linear pair postulate  ·  segment addition postulate  ·  angle addition postulate  ·  auxiliary line

- **Activity 3.GS: Getting Started: The Perfect Square** — Students attempt to freehand sketch a perfect square without tools, compare their sketches with classmates, develop criteria for evaluating which square is most perfect, and list known properties of squares such as congruent sides, right angles, parallel opposite sides, and perpendicular consecutive sides.
  - *diagram: freehand student sketches of squares | concepts: properties of squares, sketch vs. draw, congruent sides, right angles, parallel sides, perpendicular sides*
- **Activity 3.1: Mathematical Reasoning** — Students use word fragment analysis (prefixes in-, de-, root -duc-, suffix -tion) to construct the terms induction and deduction. They read definitions of inductive and deductive reasoning and apply them to two scenarios: Chloe generalizing a rule about exponents from specific examples (inductive) and Juan applying a known rule to a specific example (deductive).
  - *diagram: none | concepts: inductive reasoning, deductive reasoning, conjecture, generalizing from patterns, applying rules to specific cases*
- **Activity 3.2: Creating Mathematical Ideas** — Students learn the definition of angle and its naming conventions (by vertex, by three points, by number). They sketch and label examples of the linear pair postulate, segment addition postulate, and angle addition postulate, writing hypothesis and conclusion for each. They distinguish between postulate, theorem, and conjecture and discuss how conjectures that are proven become theorems.
  - *diagram: labeled angle diagrams, linear pair diagram, collinear points diagram for segment addition, angle with interior ray for angle addition | concepts: angle, vertex of an angle, linear pair postulate, segment addition postulate, angle addition postulate, postulate vs. theorem vs. conjecture, hypothesis, conclusion, supplementary angles*
- **Activity 3.3: Analyzing a Diagram** — Students analyze a diagram composed of three adjacent congruent squares. They draw line segments (diagonals) from the top-left corner to each internal vertex, label the three angles formed (a, b, c), and use a protractor to measure each angle. They compare results with classmates and observe that the angle measures appear to sum to 90°.
  - *diagram: three adjacent congruent squares with diagonals drawn from top-left corner to each bottom-right internal vertex | concepts: diagonal, angle measurement with protractor, conjecture from measurement, adjacent squares, angle sum, sketch vs. draw*
- **Activity 3.4: Making Conjectures** — Students evaluate Mia's conjecture that for any three adjacent congruent squares with the same diagonals drawn, the sum of angles a, b, and c is always 90°. They predict whether square size affects the angle sum, then verify by measuring angles in a larger version of the diagram. Students use patty paper to trace and physically combine the three angles to confirm their sum equals 90° without measuring.
  - *diagram: larger version of three adjacent congruent squares with diagonals; patty paper angle manipulation | concepts: conjecture, inductive reasoning, angle sum conjecture, patty paper exploration, generalization, diagonal, informal proof*
- **Activity 3.5: Drawing Auxiliary Lines** — Students examine an expanded diagram created by translating the original three squares upward to create a 2×3 grid with auxiliary line segments drawn. They analyze the resulting triangles (△AIK, △AEG, △KLH), identify right triangles formed at square corners, apply the Pythagorean Theorem to compare hypotenuse lengths, and reason deductively about angle relationships to move toward formally verifying the 90° angle sum conjecture.
  - *diagram: 2×3 grid of congruent squares with auxiliary dashed line segments creating overlapping right triangles | concepts: auxiliary line, right triangle, Pythagorean Theorem, deductive reasoning, isosceles triangle, angle relationships, translation, formal proof reasoning*
- **Activity 3.TT: Talk the Talk: Proving Yourself** — Students complete a graphic organizer distinguishing between undefined terms, definitions, postulates, theorems, and conjectures, providing a description and an example of each category.
  - *diagram: graphic organizer table | concepts: undefined term, definition, postulate, theorem, conjecture, distinguishing geometric reasoning categories*

#### Topic 2: Using a Rectangular Coordinate System

> Students construct a coordinate plane using squares and rigid motions, derive the slope, distance, and midpoint formulas, and use these tools to classify quadrilaterals and triangles on the coordinate plane. They also calculate perimeter and area of polygons and composite figures, and explore proportional and non-proportional dimensional changes.

**Lesson 1: Constructing a Coordinate Plane**

*How can you construct a coordinate plane using squares and rigid motion transformations?*

Key terms: arc  ·  midpoint  ·  segment bisector  ·  perpendicular bisector  ·  transformation  ·  rigid motion

- **Activity Getting Started: Constructing a Perpendicular Line Using Patty Paper** — Students view the coordinate plane as composed of two perpendicular lines and uniform squares. They use patty paper to fold and create a line perpendicular to a given segment, identifying the midpoint and segment bisector.
  - *diagram: line segment on patty paper with fold crease showing perpendicular bisector | concepts: perpendicular lines, segment bisector, midpoint, patty paper construction*
- **Activity 1.1: Constructing a Perpendicular Line Using a Compass and a Straightedge** — Students analyze Worked Examples demonstrating how to construct a segment bisector, a perpendicular line through a point on a line, and a perpendicular line through a point not on a line using a compass and straightedge. They conjecture about the distance from any point on a perpendicular bisector to the endpoints of the segment.
  - *diagram: compass-and-straightedge constructions of perpendicular bisectors and perpendicular lines | concepts: perpendicular bisector construction, segment bisector construction, perpendicular line through a point on a line, perpendicular line through a point not on a line, equidistant points on perpendicular bisector*
- **Activity 1.2: Constructing a Square** — Students analyze a Worked Example demonstrating duplication of a line segment with compass and straightedge. They practice this construction, construct a square with a given side length, and make conjectures about the diagonals of a square using patty paper.
  - *diagram: compass-and-straightedge construction of a square with diagonals drawn | concepts: congruent segment construction, square construction, diagonals of a square, perpendicular diagonals, bisecting diagonals*
- **Activity 1.3: Rigid Motions** — Students recall definitions of rigid motion, translation, reflection, and rotation. They analyze a strategy using translations to construct a coordinate plane from a square, describe other rigid motion strategies, and identify coordinates of vertices of shapes formed by transformations of line segments on the coordinate plane.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with two-dimensional figures formed by rigid motion transformations of line segments | concepts: translation, reflection, rotation, rigid motion, constructing coordinate plane from a square, vertices of transformed figures*
- **Activity Talk the Talk: Walking on a Thin Line** — Students describe a sequence of translations on a given line segment that produces a line, relate the translations to the slope of the line, write the line's equation in slope-intercept form, and construct a perpendicular bisector and a congruent line segment.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with a line and slope triangles from translation sequence | concepts: translation sequence and slope relationship, slope-intercept form, perpendicular bisector construction, congruent segment construction*

**Lesson 2: Parallel and Perpendicular Lines**

*How can the coordinate plane be used to justify parallel and perpendicular line relationships?*

- **Activity Getting Started: Which Way Is Up?** — Students sketch lines with positive and negative slopes off the coordinate plane, describe their characteristics, and compare sketches with classmates.
  - *diagram: freehand sketches of lines with positive and negative slopes | concepts: positive slope, negative slope, slope steepness*
- **Activity 2.1: Deriving the Slope Formula** — Students derive the slope of specific lines on the coordinate plane by calculating rise and run from coordinates, then derive the general slope formula using two arbitrary points.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with labeled lines, rise-run triangles, and general slope derivation diagram | concepts: slope formula derivation, rise over run, slope of a line through two points, general slope formula*
- **Activity 2.2: Constructing Parallel Lines** — Students construct a line parallel to a given line using three methods: constructing two lines perpendicular to the same line, constructing congruent alternate interior angles (including duplicating an angle with compass and straightedge), and using translations. They graph parallel lines on the coordinate plane and describe the slope relationship.
  - *diagram: compass-and-straightedge construction of parallel lines using perpendicular lines and angle duplication; coordinate plane graph of parallel lines | concepts: parallel line construction using perpendicular lines, angle duplication construction, parallel line construction using translations, alternate interior angles, slopes of parallel lines are equal*
- **Activity 2.3: Slopes of Perpendicular Lines** — Students identify perpendicular lines on the coordinate plane by writing their equations, then analyze a Worked Example using a 90° counterclockwise rotation to prove that slopes of perpendicular lines are negative reciprocals. They verify with a clockwise rotation and apply the relationship.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with perpendicular lines and 90-degree rotation diagram showing slope triangles | concepts: slopes of perpendicular lines, negative reciprocal slopes, 90-degree rotation proof of perpendicularity, product of perpendicular slopes equals negative one*
- **Activity 2.4: Writing Equations of Parallel and Perpendicular Lines** — Students extend their understanding to horizontal and vertical lines, determine that horizontal lines have slope zero and vertical lines have undefined slope, and write equations of lines parallel or perpendicular to given lines through given points.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with horizontal, vertical, parallel, and perpendicular lines and labeled points | concepts: slope of horizontal line, slope of vertical line, equation of parallel line through a point, equation of perpendicular line through a point, point-slope form*
- **Activity Talk the Talk: Parallels the Lesson** — Students use deductive reasoning and translations to write an explanation justifying the conjecture that if two lines are parallel, their slopes are equal. They include a sketch and construct a line parallel to a given line.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with two parallel lines and slope triangles illustrating translation argument | concepts: parallel lines have equal slopes proof, translation preserves slope, parallel line construction*

**Lesson 3: Classifying Quadrilaterals on the Coordinate Plane**

*How can you use slope, distance, and midpoint formulas to classify polygons on a coordinate plane?*

Key terms: distance formula  ·  extracting perfect squares  ·  radical  ·  radicand  ·  midpoint formula

- **Activity Getting Started: You Better Shape Up** — Students match polygons to regions of a Venn diagram based on shared properties (parallel sides, perpendicular sides, congruent sides), interpret the overlapping region of a second Venn diagram, and determine whether statements about polygons are always, sometimes, or never true.
  - *diagram: Venn diagram with three overlapping circles representing polygon properties | concepts: classifying quadrilaterals by properties, Venn diagram for polygon classification, parallelogram, rhombus, rectangle, square, trapezoid, isosceles triangle, equilateral triangle*
- **Activity 3.1: Calculating Distance on the Coordinate Plane** — Students classify a quadrilateral formed by vertical and horizontal segments, use slope criteria to classify a second quadrilateral as a parallelogram and rectangle, use one side as a hypotenuse to apply the Pythagorean Theorem, and derive the distance formula. They use the formula to classify a quadrilateral as a square and simplify radicals using prime factorization.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with quadrilaterals, right triangles drawn from sides, and labeled coordinate points for distance formula derivation | concepts: distance formula derivation from Pythagorean Theorem, distance formula application, classifying quadrilaterals using distance and slope, simplest radical form, extracting perfect squares*
- **Activity 3.2: Using the Distance Formula to Verify the Congruence of Segments** — Students translate a line segment into all four quadrants of the coordinate plane and use the distance formula to verify that translations preserve segment length, concluding that translated segments are congruent to the original.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with pre-image and image segments in all four quadrants after repeated translations | concepts: distance formula to verify segment congruence, translations preserve segment length, rigid motion and congruence*
- **Activity 3.3: Using the Distance Formula to Verify Parallelism** — Students write equations of parallel and perpendicular lines on a coordinate plane, find intersection points, use the distance formula to calculate lengths of perpendicular segments between parallel lines, and verify that two lines are parallel by showing equal perpendicular distances.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with two pairs of parallel lines intersected by perpendicular lines, labeled intersection points | concepts: distance between parallel lines, perpendicular segment between parallel lines, verifying parallelism using distance formula*
- **Activity 3.4: Classifying a Quadrilateral on the Coordinate Plane** — Students graph four points, calculate side lengths using the distance formula, determine slopes of all sides, and use these to classify the quadrilateral as a trapezoid, rhombus, rectangle, square, or none of these.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with graphed quadrilateral vertices and labeled side lengths and slopes | concepts: classifying quadrilaterals using distance formula and slope formula, rhombus identification, trapezoid identification*
- **Activity 3.5: Classifying a Quadrilateral Formed by Midpoints** — Students derive the midpoint formula algebraically, use it to find midpoints of each side of a square, connect midpoints to form a secondary figure, classify it, and repeat the process. They sketch a rhombus, connect midpoints, classify the resulting rectangle, and predict further iterations.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with square and secondary figure formed by connecting midpoints; separate rhombus with midpoint rectangle | concepts: midpoint formula derivation, midpoint formula application, connecting midpoints of quadrilateral sides, secondary figures formed by midpoints*
- **Activity Talk the Talk: Look, Ma! No Gridlines!** — Students find the fourth point of a parallelogram given three points, determine the perpendicular distance between two parallel lines, and identify a coordinate to move a point so that the resulting quadrilateral is a parallelogram.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with parallelogram vertices and perpendicular distance segment labeled | concepts: distance between parallel lines, fourth vertex of a parallelogram, parallel sides and slope, translations to identify parallelogram coordinates*

**Lesson 4: Classifying Triangles on the Coordinate Plane**

*How can you use the distance formula and slope formula to classify triangles on the coordinate plane?*

Key terms: biconditional statement  ·  converse  ·  Pythagorean triple

- **Activity Getting Started: The Converse of a Conditional Statement** — Students identify the hypothesis and conclusion of conditional statements and write the converse of each, determining whether the converse is true.
  - *diagram: none (logical reasoning activity with text statements) | concepts: conditional statement, hypothesis, conclusion, converse of a conditional statement*
- **Activity 4.1: Triple Play** — Students write the converse of the Pythagorean Theorem, combine the theorem and its converse into a biconditional statement, and use the converse to determine whether triangles with given side lengths are right triangles.
  - *diagram: right triangle diagrams with labeled side lengths | concepts: converse of the Pythagorean Theorem, biconditional statement, identifying right triangles from side lengths*
- **Activity 4.2: Pythagorean Triples** — Students investigate Pythagorean triples, generate new triples by multiplying a known triple by a scale factor, complete tables of triples, and apply Pythagorean triples to solve real-world problems involving rectangle diagonals.
  - *diagram: right triangle diagrams with integer side lengths representing Pythagorean triples | concepts: Pythagorean triple, generating Pythagorean triples using scale factor, applying Pythagorean triples to real-world problems*
- **Activity 4.3: Using the Distance Formula to Verify Perpendicularity** — Students apply the distance formula to calculate the three side lengths of a triangle on the coordinate plane and use the converse of the Pythagorean Theorem to verify that two of the sides are perpendicular.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with triangle formed by two potentially perpendicular line segments | concepts: distance formula to verify perpendicularity, converse of Pythagorean Theorem application, perpendicular segments on coordinate plane*
- **Activity 4.4: Classifying Triangles on the Coordinate Plane** — Students use the distance formula to classify triangles as scalene, isosceles, or equilateral, use the slope formula to determine whether a triangle is a right triangle, use the Pythagorean Theorem to verify right triangles, and use side length relationships to classify triangles as acute, right, or obtuse.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with graphed triangles, labeled vertices, and slope and distance calculations | concepts: classifying triangles by side length using distance formula, right triangle identification using slope, acute triangle classification, obtuse triangle classification, Pythagorean Theorem for triangle classification*
- **Activity Talk the Talk: Tri- Tri- Again** — Students apply Pythagorean triples to solve a real-world problem involving a rectangular sign diagonal, and classify a triangle on the coordinate plane by its sides (isosceles, scalene, equilateral) and angles (right, acute, obtuse).
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with graphed triangle vertices and classification annotations | concepts: Pythagorean triples application, classifying triangles on coordinate plane by sides and angles*

**Lesson 5: Area and Perimeter on the Coordinate Plane**

*How can you use the distance formula and slope formula to determine the perimeter and area of polygons on the coordinate plane?*

Key terms: composite figure  ·  regular polygon  ·  apothem

- **Activity Getting Started: Perimeter and Area of Figures on the Coordinate Plane** — Students calculate the perimeter and area of a rectangle on the coordinate plane, investigate how doubling one dimension affects perimeter and area, and explore how translating the rectangle to place a vertex at the origin makes calculations more efficient.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with a rectangle not aligned to axes, and translated image with vertex at origin | concepts: perimeter of rectangle on coordinate plane, area of rectangle on coordinate plane, effect of doubling one dimension on perimeter and area, translation for computational efficiency*
- **Activity 5.1: Using the Distance Formula to Determine Perimeter and Area on the Coordinate Plane** — Students calculate the perimeter and area of a square and right triangle on the coordinate plane using the distance formula, describe the effect of doubling one or both dimensions, analyze different rigid motion strategies to make calculations efficient, and verify that the height of a triangle must be perpendicular to the base.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with squares and triangles not aligned to axes; translated images of triangles for efficiency | concepts: distance formula for perimeter, area of square on coordinate plane, area of triangle on coordinate plane, effect of doubling dimensions on area and perimeter, rigid motions for computational efficiency, perpendicular height of a triangle*
- **Activity 5.2: Proportional and Non-Proportional Dimensions Changes** — Students complete tables comparing perimeter and area of rectangles after doubling or tripling dimensions proportionally, generalize the effect of a scale factor k on perimeter (factor of k) and area (factor of k²), then repeat the investigation with non-proportional changes to one or both dimensions.
  - *diagram: rectangles with labeled dimensions showing proportional and non-proportional scaling | concepts: proportional dimensional change effect on perimeter, proportional dimensional change effect on area, non-proportional dimensional change, scale factor k and perimeter, scale factor k squared and area*
- **Activity 5.3: Calculating Heights of Triangles** — Students calculate the area of a triangle with no horizontal or vertical sides by algebraically determining the height. A Worked Example shows determining the height by writing equations of the base line and the perpendicular height line, solving the system to find the foot of the height, and using the distance formula. Students solve for the area using each of the three sides as the base, concluding the area is the same regardless of the base chosen.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with non-axis-aligned triangle, perpendicular height segment from vertex to base line, and system of equations | concepts: non-vertical height of a triangle, perpendicular height to non-horizontal base, equation of line containing base, equation of height line, system of equations to find foot of altitude, area of triangle independent of base choice*
- **Activity 5.4: Perimeter and Area of a Composite Figure** — Students are introduced to composite figures. Given a composite figure representing an irregular backyard on a coordinate plane, they calculate perimeter using the distance formula and area by decomposing the figure into rectangles and triangles. They compare methods and apply unit conversions to solve a real-world cost problem.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with an irregular composite polygon (combination of rectangles and triangles) representing a backyard | concepts: composite figure, perimeter of composite figure using distance formula, area of composite figure by decomposition, decomposing into rectangles and triangles, real-world perimeter and area application*
- **Activity 5.5: Solving Problems with Perimeter and Area** — Students calculate the shaded and unshaded areas of a composite figure (kite inside a rectangle), determine the perimeter and area of a composite wall figure on the coordinate plane for a real-world painting scenario, and calculate the perimeter and area of a hexagonal gazebo base by decomposing the hexagon into triangles and a rectangle.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with kite inside rectangle; composite wall figure (rectangle with triangular top); hexagon decomposed into triangles and rectangle | concepts: area of kite, composite figure area subtraction, perimeter of composite figure, area of composite figure, decomposing hexagon into triangles and rectangles, real-world area and perimeter problems*
- **Activity 5.6: Areas of Regular Polygons** — Students develop a strategy for calculating the area of regular polygons by decomposing them into n congruent triangles, derive the formula A = (1/2)aP where a is the apothem and P is the perimeter, and apply the formula to calculate areas of regular polygons and composite figures containing regular polygons.
  - *diagram: regular pentagon and other regular polygons decomposed into congruent triangles from center, with apothem labeled | concepts: regular polygon, decomposing regular polygon into congruent triangles, apothem, area of regular polygon formula A = (1/2)aP, applying regular polygon area formula*
- **Activity Talk the Talk: Shape It Up** — Students determine the area of the shaded region within a regular octagon with an octagonal hole, evaluate whether a composite classroom design meets perimeter and area requirements, and analyze the effect of dimensional changes on a rectangle's perimeter and area.
  - *diagram: regular octagon with smaller octagonal hole; composite classroom floor plan on coordinate plane; rectangle with dimensional change annotations | concepts: area of regular polygon with hole, composite figure perimeter and area evaluation, proportional vs. non-proportional dimensional change effects*

#### Topic 3: Sequences of Rigid Motions

> Students build on their understanding of rigid motion transformations (translations, reflections, and rotations) on and off the coordinate plane, performing single and sequences of transformations, constructing angle bisectors and lines of reflection, and using compositions of rigid motions to prove two figures are congruent.

**Lesson 1: Translations On and Off the Coordinate Plane**

*How can you describe geometric translations of figures on and off the coordinate plane?*

Key terms: coordinate notation  ·  congruent angles  ·  congruent triangles  ·  translation

- **Activity Getting Started: Translating Geometric Figures on the Coordinate Plane** — Students cut out or trace a trapezoid onto patty paper, graph it on a coordinate plane, translate it horizontally and vertically, record vertex coordinates, and write rules in coordinate notation for the translations.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with trapezoid and translated images | concepts: translation on coordinate plane, coordinate notation, horizontal and vertical translation, vertex coordinates*
- **Activity 1.1: Translation Functions** — Students analyze a Worked Example using a directed line segment to complete a translation of a triangle on a website banner, describe translation functions, complete translations using function descriptions, and write congruence and equality statements for pre-image and image triangles.
  - *diagram: triangles with directed line segments on and off coordinate plane | concepts: directed line segment, translation function, parallel lines, congruent triangles, congruent angles, rigid motion transformation*
- **Activity 1.2: Determining Congruence Using Translations** — Students describe and draw translation functions mapping triangles on a menu web page, determine congruence of translated figures, and distinguish rigid motion transformations from dilations using a non-congruent example.
  - *diagram: triangles with directed line segments; dilation comparison diagram | concepts: translation function, rigid motion transformation, dilation, congruent figures, non-rigid transformation*
- **Activity Talk the Talk: Rigid Motions on the Menu** — Students describe translation functions for given pre-image/image pairs of a trapezoid and a triangle, then compare geometric translation functions to algebraic equations representing translation of a function.
  - *diagram: trapezoid and triangle with directed line segments | concepts: translation function, directed line segment, rigid motion, algebraic vs. geometric translation*

**Lesson 2: Sequences of Multiple Translations**

*How can you describe a translation by a directed line segment on and off the coordinate plane using a sequence of transformations?*

- **Activity Getting Started: Multiple Translations on the Coordinate Plane** — Students examine a trapezoid and its translated image, describe a single directed line segment translation, calculate segment lengths using the distance formula, describe the equivalent sequence of horizontal and vertical translations, use the Pythagorean Theorem to relate the directed line segment length to the translations, and describe a sequence of translations off the coordinate plane for a triangle.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with trapezoid ABCD and image A'B'C'D'; off-coordinate-plane triangle diagram | concepts: directed line segment, sequence of translations, Pythagorean Theorem, horizontal and vertical translation, translation off coordinate plane*
- **Activity 2.1: Sequences of Translations Using Coordinate Notation** — Students record coordinates of pre-image and image triangles, describe and write rules for sequences of translations in coordinate notation, determine image and pre-image coordinates without graphing, write equality and congruence statements, and write triangle congruence statements.
  - *diagram: coordinate plane with triangles and trapezoids before and after sequences of translations | concepts: sequence of translations, coordinate notation, composition of rigid transformations, congruence statements, image and pre-image coordinates*
- **Activity Talk the Talk: Off the Grid** — Students describe and write rules for a sequence of translations mapping a pre-image triangle to an image on the coordinate plane, then describe the same mapping off the coordinate plane using a directed line segment, and write congruency statements.
  - *diagram: triangle on coordinate plane and triangle off coordinate plane | concepts: sequence of translations, coordinate notation, directed line segment, congruency statements, on and off coordinate plane comparison*

**Lesson 3: Reflections On and Off the Coordinate Plane**

*How can you describe geometric reflections of figures on the plane?*

Key terms: reflection  ·  angle bisector  ·  perpendicular bisector theorem
