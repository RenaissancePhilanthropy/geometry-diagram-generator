\section{Human-correlation study materials and results}
\label{app:human_study}

This appendix accompanies the headline trust claim that the \GeoGenBench automatic verdict agrees with expert human judgment (\cref{ssec:human}, \cref{tab:trust_claims}).
The pre-registered protocol was committed to the project repository at \texttt{docs/human\_study\_protocol.md} \emph{before} annotation began; this appendix is its paper-facing counterpart.
We separate \textbf{methodology} (frozen pre-collection) from \textbf{results} (filled at study completion) to make the pre-registration auditable: changes to the methodology subsections after \texttt{2026-05-02} are logged in the protocol's \emph{deviation log} and surfaced here.

\subsection{Raters}
\label{app:human_study:raters}

Two domain-expert raters annotate the full sample independently:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Rater A}: PhD in mathematics; co-author.
\item \textbf{Rater B}: award-winning K--12 mathematics educator with middle-school teaching experience; co-author.
\end{itemize}

Both raters are paper authors --- a real but standard limitation for benchmark papers --- which we mitigate by (a)~Q1 blinding, (b)~independent annotation prior to consensus, and (c)~reporting per-rater $\kappa$ against the auto-verdict alongside the consensus statistic so reviewers can read how much consensus discussion shifted the headline number.
Post-acceptance work (\cref{sec:conclusion}) extends the rater pool to a heterogeneous external sample.

\subsection{Sample construction ($N = 200$)}
\label{app:human_study:sample}

The sample is drawn deterministically (random seed 42) from the canonical post-hardening pilot \texttt{evals/results/leaderboard\_pilot\_v3} (498 records), restricted to the two strategies reported in the headline run (\rawcode, \structured).
Of the 348 records under those strategies, 55 lack a rendered SVG (all are \texttt{gate\_status\,=\,fail} with unparseable model output: there is no diagram to rate).
The sampling pool is therefore 293 records:

\begin{itemize}
\item All \textbf{28 \texttt{soft\_pass}} records (verifier was uncertain --- the most epistemically informative stratum).
\item All \textbf{21 rendered-but-failed} records (the entire pool of cases where the verifier said \texttt{fail} but a diagram exists for human inspection).
\item \textbf{151 \texttt{pass}} records sampled from the remaining 244, balanced across (tier $\times$ strategy $\times$ model). Cells with fewer records than the per-cell budget are taken in full and the deficit is redistributed to the largest cells.
\end{itemize}

The realised composition (deterministic given the seed) is summarised in \cref{tab:human_sample_composition}.
The sample is committed at \texttt{evals/human\_study/sample.json}; running \texttt{python -m evals.human\_study.sample\_human\_study} reproduces it byte-identically.

\subsection{Rubric}
\label{app:human_study:rubric}

Each item presents the rater with the natural-language prompt and the rendered SVG, and (for Q2 only, after Q1 is submitted) the verifier's per-predicate verdict.

\paragraph{Q1 --- Overall verdict (blind, mandatory).}
\textit{``Does this diagram correctly satisfy every claim in the prompt?''}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Yes} --- every prompt clause is geometrically satisfied.
\item \textbf{Partial} --- some clauses are satisfied, some are violated.
\item \textbf{No} --- at least one major prompt clause is violated, or the diagram is unrelated to the prompt.
\end{itemize}
For the binary collapse used in the primary endpoint, \emph{Partial} maps to \emph{No} (conservative; treats partial satisfaction as failure of the per-prompt completeness criterion).

\paragraph{Q2 --- Per-predicate agreement (post-Q1, mandatory per row).}
For each predicate the verifier checked on the diagram (mean $4.4$ per item), the rater records:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Agrees-pass} --- verifier said pass and the property holds in the diagram.
\item \textbf{Agrees-fail} --- verifier said fail and the property does not hold.
\item \textbf{Disagrees} --- verifier said the opposite of what the rater observes.
\item \textbf{Unsure} --- the diagram is geometrically ambiguous on this property.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Optional notes.} A free-text field per item for disagreement-coding observations (``verifier's tolerance was too loose'', ``rater missed an implicit prompt constraint until consensus discussion'', etc.); these feed the qualitative coding in \cref{app:human_study:disagreement_coding}.

\subsection{Workflow and tooling}
\label{app:human_study:tooling}

The rating tool is a single static HTML page (\texttt{evals/human\_study/viewer.html}) served by a small local Python server (\texttt{evals/human\_study/serve.py}) that auto-saves answers to a per-rater CSV (\texttt{responses\_<rater>.csv}) on every keystroke.
Q1 is rendered \emph{without} the auto-verdict or any predicate values; Q2 unlocks only after Q1 is submitted, mechanically enforcing the blinding protocol.
Keyboard-only operation is supported throughout (\texttt{1/2/3} for Q1, \texttt{a/f/d/u} for Q2, $\rightarrow / \leftarrow$ for navigation).

After both raters complete independent annotation, \texttt{python -m evals.human\_study.compute\_kappa} reads both response CSVs, computes inter-rater Cohen's $\kappa$, and emits a disagreement list.
The two raters then meet in a single $\leq 90$-minute session to discuss each disagreement to convergence and produce a consensus label per item; the consensus labels are saved to \texttt{responses\_consensus.csv}.
The same script re-runs against the consensus labels to produce the headline $\kappa(\text{auto},\text{consensus})$ and the analyses below.

\subsection{Pre-registered analyses}
\label{app:human_study:analyses}

\paragraph{Primary endpoint.}
Cohen's $\kappa(\text{auto-verdict}, \text{human-consensus}) \geq 0.70$ on the binary collapse (\texttt{pass}~$\cup$~\texttt{soft\_pass} vs \texttt{fail}), $N = 200$.
We report the point estimate, an asymptotic 95\% CI (Fleiss--Cohen--Everitt), and a 1000-resample bootstrap 95\% CI; the protocol pre-registers the bootstrap CI's lower bound exceeding $0.70$ as the formal pass criterion.
We additionally report balanced accuracy of the auto-verdict against the consensus label.

\paragraph{Secondary analyses (reported regardless of primary outcome).}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Inter-rater reliability}: Cohen's $\kappa$ between the two human raters on Q1 binary and on Q1 three-way (\texttt{yes}/\texttt{partial}/\texttt{no}), computed before consensus discussion.
\item \textbf{Per-tier $\kappa$}: $\kappa(\text{auto},\text{consensus})$ separately on T1, T2, T3.
\item \textbf{Per-strategy $\kappa$}: separately on \rawcode, \structured.
\item \textbf{Per-vendor $\kappa$}: separately for Anthropic, OpenAI, Google models (per-cell N is too small for per-model splits at this sample size).
\item \textbf{Per-rater independent $\kappa$}: $\kappa(\text{auto},\text{rater~A})$ and $\kappa(\text{auto},\text{rater~B})$ separately, as a robustness check on how much consensus discussion shifted the headline.
\item \textbf{Per-predicate Q2 agreement rate}: agreement rate broken down by each of the 17 implemented predicates.
\item \textbf{Disagreement coding}: consensus-discussion notes coded by category and counts reported (\cref{app:human_study:disagreement_coding}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Acknowledged limitations}
\label{app:human_study:limitations}

\paragraph{Two raters, not three.}
Standard inter-rater statistics for $\geq 3$ raters (Fleiss's $\kappa$, intraclass correlation) are not applicable; we report Cohen's pairwise $\kappa$.
Two raters is sufficient to estimate $\kappa(\text{auto}, \text{consensus})$ at the pre-registered precision but provides only one inter-rater datum.

\paragraph{Raters are paper authors.}
Conflict of interest is real and material.
We mitigate via (a)~Q1 blinding to the auto-verdict and to the per-predicate values, (b)~independent annotation before any consensus discussion, and (c)~reporting per-rater $\kappa$ against the auto-verdict alongside the consensus statistic so the contribution of consensus discussion to the headline is auditable.
Post-acceptance work extends to an external rater pool drawn from the K--12 mathematics-education community.

\paragraph{Templated split only.}
The pilot (\texttt{leaderboard\_pilot\_v3}) does not include curriculum-extracted scenarios, so all 200 sample items are templated.
Curriculum-style prompts have a different failure mode profile (LLM-extracted property lists may be incomplete; templated property lists are guaranteed-by-construction); the human-correlation generalisation across this gap is queued for follow-up.

\paragraph{Three-day data-collection window.}
Annotation occurs over 2026-05-02 to 2026-05-05; a typical item receives 2--4 minutes of rater attention.
This is consistent with the inter-rater workflows used in published benchmark papers but is shorter than expert deliberation in adjudicated educational assessment.
