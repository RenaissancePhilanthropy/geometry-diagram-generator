\begin{abstract}
Existing geometry benchmarks ask language models to \emph{interpret} a diagram and answer a question.
None ask whether a model can \emph{produce} a correct diagram from a description.
We introduce \textbf{\GeoGen}, a methodology for automatically-verifiable benchmarks of generative geometric reasoning, and \textbf{\GeoGenBench}, its first instance: 801 prompts paired with machine-checkable property lists from a 17-predicate language.
\GeoGen co-generates prompts and rubrics from shared Python source (eliminating drift between question and grading criteria), then renders the model's TikZ output and decides each predicate symbolically against compiled SymPy coordinates --- no human grading, no vision--language judge.

We benchmark six frontier models from three vendors under raw-TikZ and structured-IR strategies.
\textbf{The cost--quality Pareto frontier collapses to a single corner.}
Under the IR strategy, \gpt\ ($94.1$\%, \dollars{0.075}/scen), \sonnet\ ($93.4$\%, \dollars{0.088}), and \opus\ ($93.3$\%, \dollars{0.532}) tie within $1$\pp at a $\mathbf{7\times}$ cost spread.
On raw TikZ, \gpt\ ($91.9$\%, \dollars{0.058}) beats \opus\ ($71.6$\%, \dollars{0.262}) by $20$\pp at $4.5\times$ lower cost.
The IR adds $2$--$25$\pp over raw TikZ, largest for the models weakest at raw.
One vendor (Gemini Flash) rejects the IR as exceeding its structured-output complexity cap --- a portability gap, not a capability gap.
A pre-registered $N{=}200$ human-correlation study finds moderate auto/expert agreement ($\kappa{=}0.455$) with one-directional verifier lenience: $\sim 18\%$ of \texttt{pass} records overturn to expert \texttt{fail}, zero the reverse, concentrated on templates whose property lists do not enforce prompt-specified numerics.
We release the dataset, the template engine, the verification toolkit, and a public leaderboard.
\end{abstract}
