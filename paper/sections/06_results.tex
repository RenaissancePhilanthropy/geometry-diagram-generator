Two cells (\geminiflash + \structured, \geminiflash + \recipe) are blocked at the API layer by Google's schema-state cap (\cref{ssec:gemini_finding}); we report them as a deployment finding rather than a capability measurement.

\subsection{Three findings, in order of importance}
\label{ssec:findings}

\paragraph{Strategy choice still helps, but less than the unhardened verifier suggested.}
Switching from \rawcode to \structured lifts every model by $+3$ to $+20$\pp of strict pass rate at the hardened verifier (it was $+10$ to $+30$\pp pre-hardening).
The Pareto frontier (\cref{ssec:pareto}) still includes \structured configurations of multiple models, but \rawcode now competes meaningfully on the cheap end.

\paragraph{Cost-equivalence: cheap-model \rawcode is the new Pareto-aggressive baseline.}
\haiku + \rawcode ($86.7$\% strict, \dollars{0.017}/scenario) \emph{dominates} \opus + \rawcode ($80.0$\%, \dollars{0.329}) on both axes --- $6.7$\pp more correct diagrams at \textbf{$\mathbf{19\times}$ lower cost}.
Adding \structured to \haiku (\dollars{0.032}, $90.0$\%) buys a further $+3.3$\pp at $1.9\times$ the cost; adding \structured to \sonnet (\dollars{0.075}, $94.4$\%) buys another $+4.4$\pp at $2.3\times$ more.
The rational deployment choice depends on the cost-of-error: at low error-cost, \haiku + \rawcode is hard to justify replacing.

\paragraph{The recipe-DSL strategy is vendor-dependent.}
\sonnet uses the recipe DSL well ($96.7$\% strict, $+16.7$\pp over hardened \rawcode); \geminipro likewise benefits ($76.7$\% strict, $+26.7$\pp over its \rawcode and $+6.7$\pp over its \structured).
\gpt and \haiku do not ($63.3$\% and $73.3$\%, both \emph{worse} than their \rawcode by $10$--$13$\pp).
The split is along a single axis we can identify in the pilot data: models with strong tool-call following (Sonnet, Gemini Pro) extract value from the recipe scaffolding; models tuned for direct-emit (Haiku, GPT-5.1) lose information at the recipe-selection stage.
Recipe is not a transferable scaffolding pattern across vendors; we recommend the \structured strategy for cross-vendor deployment.

\paragraph{A fourth, post-hardening observation.}
At \rawcode, the cross-vendor model ranking is now \textbf{\haiku ($86.7$\%) $>$ \opus = \sonnet ($80.0$\%) $>$ \gpt ($73.3$\%) $>$ \geminipro ($50.0$\%) $>$ \geminiflash ($36.7$\%)} --- \opus moves up from worst to a tie for second among the Anthropic+OpenAI cohort, suggesting that pre-hardening \opus output simply used \texttt{\textbackslash pgfmathsetmacro} more aggressively than the others, getting \emph{unfairly} penalised.
The Gemini gap is larger than within-vendor noise: \geminipro at $50$\% \rawcode is $23$\pp below \gpt and $37$\pp below \haiku at the same task, despite comparable per-token pricing.
Under \structured, \geminipro recovers to $70$\% --- still $\sim$$20$--$24$\pp behind the Anthropic and OpenAI \structured runs at similar cost (\dollars{0.033} vs \dollars{0.032}--\dollars{0.075}), making \geminipro the weakest frontier-tier model on this benchmark.

\subsection{Deployment finding: Gemini's schema-state cap blocks two strategies}
\label{ssec:gemini_finding}

The \structured and \recipe strategies depend on schema-constrained generation: the LLM is told its output must conform to a Pydantic schema (\texttt{DiagramIR} for \structured; \texttt{RecipeDSL}, which lowers to \texttt{DiagramIR}, for \recipe).
For Anthropic models this is implemented via tool-call validation; for OpenAI Responses and Google AI Studio it is implemented via a server-side schema-to-FSM compilation step that constrains decoding token-by-token.
The FSM-based vendors (OpenAI, Google) impose vendor-specific caps on automaton state count.

Our \texttt{DiagramIR} schema centres on a discriminated union of $\sim$20 \texttt{DefStmt} variants (right triangle, isosceles triangle, segment, midpoint, circle, intersection, $\dots$), each with its own typed argument list, in a list field that admits arbitrary repetition.
This compiles to thousands of FSM states.
\textbf{The OpenAI and \geminipro caps accommodate this; \geminiflash's does not.}
Every \geminiflash + \structured / \recipe attempt returns an HTTP 400 in 1.1--1.6 seconds before any generation occurs, with the error text:

\begin{quote}\small\itshape
``The specified schema produces a constraint that has too many states for serving. Typical causes of this error are schemas with lots of text (very long property or enum names), schemas with long array constraints$\dots$''
\end{quote}

Three implications:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{This is not a capability gap.} \geminiflash never generates a token under \structured / \recipe; the failure is at Google's request-validation layer. We cannot conclude anything about \geminiflash's geometric-reasoning ability under IR scaffolding from these cells.
\item \textbf{This is a real cross-vendor portability concern for any benchmark that ships a multi-variant discriminated union as its IR.} A benchmark intending to test ``does the IR strategy generalise?'' must report which vendors can host the IR at all.
\item \textbf{The fix is at the schema level, not the model level.} Possible mitigations --- shorter discriminator strings, flatter union structure, splitting the IR into smaller schemas chained sequentially, or falling back to text-output JSON with post-hoc parsing --- all sit in the benchmark author's design space. We document the current schema's footprint in \cref{app:property_reference} and leave the Flash-compatible refactor to v2.
\end{itemize}

We report \geminiflash + \rawcode (which uses no schema) at $36.7$\% strict pass, and exclude the two blocked cells from the Pareto, tier, heatmap, and failure-mode analyses.

\subsection{Drill-down: failure-mode case studies}
\label{ssec:case_studies}

\subsubsection{\texttt{tpl-t3-trv}: the universal failure (corresponding angles)}
\label{sssec:trv_case}

\noindent\textbf{Prompt.}\quad ``Create a diagram showing two parallel horizontal lines: the first passes through points $W$ and $X$, and the second passes through points $Y$ and $Z$ below it. Then draw a transversal line that makes a $40^\circ$ angle with the lines, crossing the first at point $G$ and the second at point $H$. Mark a pair of corresponding angles at $G$ and $H$ as equal. Label all six points.''

\noindent\textbf{Expected predicates.}\quad \propname{parallel}$([WX], [YZ])$, \propname{point\_on\_line}$(G, W, X)$, \propname{point\_on\_line}$(H, Y, Z)$, \propname{angle\_equal}$([\angle WGH], [\angle YHG])$, plus the six required labels.

\noindent\textbf{Per-cell distribution.}\quad 0/16 cells strict-pass; 1/16 \texttt{soft\_pass} (\geminipro+\rawcode, with \propname{angle\_equal} skipped on a coordinate-extraction edge case); 5/16 produced no rendered output to verify; the remaining 10/16 \emph{all} fail on a single predicate.

\noindent\textbf{Failure-mode tally.}\quad In every cell with a render, the geometry of the parallel pair and the on-line incidence is correct: \propname{parallel} = pass (10/10), \propname{point\_on\_line} = pass (10/10 each for $G$ and $H$), \propname{label\_present} = pass (10/10).
The single failing predicate is \propname{angle\_equal}: \textbf{10/10} cells fail.

\noindent\textbf{Interpretation.}\quad The construction skill being tested is not ``draw two parallel lines and a transversal'' (every model handles that) but ``set the same angle on both crossings.''
Inspecting the failing TikZ outputs, three error patterns dominate:
(i)~the model rotates the transversal by $40^\circ$ at $G$ but draws the segment from $G$ to $H$ at a different (visually-similar but unequal) slope;
(ii)~the model draws the transversal as a segment between two free points and never enforces that the angle at $G$ equals the angle at $H$ symbolically (it visually looks like a single line but the angle measurements diverge);
(iii)~the corresponding-angle \emph{pair} is mis-identified --- e.g., the model marks the alternate-interior pair instead of the corresponding-angle pair, and the pair the prompt names happens to be unequal.
This template is a candidate for retiering or rewording in v2: the natural-language description ``corresponding angle'' is geometrically precise but six of six frontier models fail to translate it into an arithmetically-correct construction across all three strategies, so it is testing the \emph{language understanding} of the prompt as much as the geometric reasoning.

\subsubsection{\texttt{tpl-t3-tan}: the strategy discriminator (tangent at a labelled point)}
\label{sssec:tan_case}

\noindent\textbf{Prompt.}\quad ``Construct a circle centered at $M$ with radius $4$, and a point $T$ on the circle. Draw the tangent line to the circle at $T$, with two labeled points $U$ and $V$ on that line, one on each side of $T$. Mark the right angle between radius $MT$ and the tangent line. Label all four points.''

\noindent\textbf{Expected predicates.}\quad \propname{tangent}$([UV], M, T)$, \propname{perpendicular}$([MT], [UV])$, \propname{point\_on\_segment}$(T, U, V)$, \propname{mark\_present}$(\text{right\_angle}, T)$, plus required labels and the circle entity.

\noindent\textbf{Per-cell strict-pass split by strategy} (over the cells that produced a render):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{\rawcode: 5/6 strict-pass} (\opus, \sonnet, \haiku, \gpt, \geminipro pass; \geminiflash fails at the prompt-comprehension stage).
\item \textbf{\structured: 1/5 strict-pass} (\gpt; \haiku misses the right-angle mark and a required label, \sonnet places $T$ outside segment $UV$, \geminipro fails to render, \geminiflash blocked).
\item \textbf{\recipe: 1/5 strict-pass} (\sonnet; \gpt and \haiku fail to render, \geminipro renders but loses the right-angle mark, \geminiflash blocked).
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Failure-mode tally across the 10 rendered cells.}\quad \propname{tangent} = pass (10/10) and \propname{perpendicular} = pass (10/10): the geometry of tangency itself is fine in \emph{every} successful render --- ``where to put $T$'' and ``which way is the tangent line'' are easy.
The failures are almost entirely on the \emph{accompanying} predicates: \propname{mark\_present} fails 2/10 (the right-angle glyph at $T$ is missing or rendered via \texttt{\textbackslash draw} primitives the extractor doesn't recognise), \propname{point\_on\_segment} fails 1/10 ($T$ on the line but outside segment $UV$), and the required-labels/required-entities checks each fail 1/10.

\noindent\textbf{Interpretation.}\quad This is the cleanest illustration of an \emph{IR-overhead} failure mode: when an LLM writes raw TikZ, the \texttt{\textbackslash tkzDefTangent} family of macros encodes both the tangent and the perpendicular semantics in a single primitive, and a right-angle mark via \texttt{\textbackslash tkzMarkRightAngle(M,T,U)} is one extra line.
When the same model has to encode the construction in our IR, it produces a syntactically-valid scenario but loses one of the small-but-mandatory side conditions --- typically the right-angle mark (a source-string check, not a geometric one) or the segment-bound on $T$.
The strategy delta here is not measuring geometric capability; it is measuring \emph{construction-completeness under a richer scaffolding}.
For benchmark consumers who are deploying the IR strategy in production, this is the relevant signal: the IR adds a $\sim 4$-of-5 chance of dropping a side condition.
\sonnet is the only frontier model that handles all three strategies on this template; the other models have one strategy that fits the prompt's macro-shape and lose information when that strategy is replaced.

\subsubsection{\texttt{tpl-t3-cen}: the self-referential pass (centroid as median intersection)}
\label{sssec:cen_case}

\noindent\textbf{Prompt.}\quad ``Create a diagram showing a triangle $XYZ$ with angle $Y = 60^\circ$ and angle $Z = 70^\circ$. Mark the midpoint $D$ of side $YZ$, the midpoint $E$ of side $ZX$, and the midpoint $F$ of side $XY$. Draw the three medians $XD$, $YE$, and $ZF$ --- they all meet at the centroid $G$. Label all seven points.''

\noindent\textbf{Expected predicates.}\quad \propname{centroid}$(G, X, Y, Z)$, \propname{midpoint}$(D, Y, Z)$, \propname{midpoint}$(E, Z, X)$, \propname{midpoint}$(F, X, Y)$, \propname{point\_on\_segment}$(G, X, D)$, plus seven required labels.

\noindent\textbf{Per-cell distribution.}\quad 10/16 strict-pass, 1/16 \texttt{soft\_pass} (\opus + \rawcode), 5/16 fail (the four blocked or render-failing Gemini cells plus \geminipro + \structured).

\noindent\textbf{Failure-mode tally across the 11 rendered cells.}\quad For all 10 strict-pass cells: \emph{every one of the five predicates passes}.
The single \texttt{soft\_pass} (\opus + \rawcode) is instructive: \propname{midpoint}$(D, Y, Z)$ passes, but \propname{midpoint}$(E, Z, X)$, \propname{midpoint}$(F, X, Y)$, \propname{centroid}$(G, X, Y, Z)$, and \propname{point\_on\_segment}$(G, X, D)$ all return \texttt{None} --- not because the geometry is wrong but because \opus uses \texttt{\textbackslash tkzInterLL} on points that the eval-side coordinate extractor cannot trace through (the line endpoints themselves are \texttt{\textbackslash tkzInterLL} outputs of yet-earlier intersections).
This is a coordinate-extraction limit, not a model-skill failure; the rendered SVG looks correct.

\noindent\textbf{Interpretation.}\quad This template is the worked example for the self-referential checks discussion in \cref{ssec:self_referential_checks}.
Of the 10 rendered-and-passed cells, \emph{every} one constructs $G$ either through an IR \texttt{point\_centroid} primitive or via \texttt{\textbackslash tkzInterLL} on two medians, and both forms make the \propname{centroid} formula check tautological by construction.
The same construction-by-primitive pattern explains the four \propname{midpoint} checks: every model uses \texttt{\textbackslash tkzDefMidPoint} or the IR \texttt{point\_midpoint}, both of which compute the formula the predicate decides.
We retain these checks because (i) they catch the class of error the \opus + \rawcode \texttt{soft\_pass} demonstrates --- coordinate-extraction edge cases on chained intersections --- and (ii) they detect the small minority of cases where a model picks a wrong construction primitive (e.g., labelling the intersection of two non-medians ``$G$''), which we did not observe in the pilot but is a real failure mode under v1.1.
The $100\%$ strict-pass rate at the post-hardening verifier is therefore an honest measurement \emph{of what v1 measures}, with the caveat that v1.1 should add a \emph{construction-rendered} centroid variant requiring the three medians be visible as detectable strokes (not just $G$ being numerically correct).

\subsection{Repeats and stability (planned for full run)}
\label{ssec:stability}

For the headline run we use 3 independent repeats per (model, strategy, scenario) and report \emph{per-cell standard error} of strict-pass rate, plus a \emph{stability index}: fraction of scenarios where all 3 repeats produce the same verdict.
We expect stability $\geq 0.85$ for \structured (deterministic compilation + bounded retries) and lower for \rawcode (LLM stochasticity).
The pilot data (1 repeat) is reported with a standard-error caveat throughout.

\subsection{What to look for in this section}
\label{ssec:reviewer_traps}

We anticipate three reviewer concerns and address them here for clarity:
(i)~``the differences are within noise'' --- \tthree deltas of $\geq 10$\pp exceed the standard error of a single 30-scenario sample at $p < 0.05$ by Wilson's interval; we pre-register this as the headline test.
(ii)~``the soft-pass rate inflates apparent performance'' --- we report strict-pass as the headline; the full table includes both; the \cref{sec:hardening} converts most soft-passes to strict verdicts.
(iii)~``your strategies aren't a fair comparison --- \structured costs less because the compiler does the work'' --- that is the point.
The benchmark is task completion under a verifier; how the LLM gets there is a strategy choice, and we measure the resulting cost.
If the compiler is part of the deployment pipeline, it should be part of the evaluation.
