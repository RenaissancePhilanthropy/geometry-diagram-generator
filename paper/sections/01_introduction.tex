\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Modern frontier LLMs are being asked to do things that were science-fiction five years ago, including authoring entire visual artifacts: slide decks, worksheets, technical illustrations, and engineering diagrams.
The most-deployed visual artifact in the K--12 educational market is the \emph{geometry diagram}: a 2D plane figure paired with a problem statement.
Yet every existing geometry benchmark we are aware of evaluates the inverse direction --- given a diagram, answer a question about it --- and provides no way to evaluate whether a model can \emph{produce} a correct diagram from a textual description.
This is the gap \GeoGenBench fills.

\subsection{Motivating example}

\begin{quote}\itshape
``Draw an acute triangle $EFG$ with vertex angles $60^\circ$, $70^\circ$ at $E$ and $F$. Construct the altitude from $G$ meeting $EF$ at $H$.''
\end{quote}
A correct rendering must satisfy: (a) $\angle E = 60^\circ$, $\angle F = 70^\circ$, (b) $H$ lies on segment $EF$ (not its extension), (c) $\angle GHE = 90^\circ$, (d) the triangle is acute.
\GeoGenBench encodes each clause as a machine-checkable predicate against the SymPy-compiled coordinates of the model's rendered TikZ output.
On this scenario (\texttt{tpl-t2-alt-EFG-H-60-70} in our pilot), \haiku and \opus pass under all three strategies; \gpt with \rawcode places $H$ on the line $EF$ but \emph{outside} the segment (extension); \sonnet with \rawcode draws an obtuse triangle.
The verifier flags each failure with a specific predicate verdict, not a holistic score, enabling actionable per-check error analysis.

\subsection{Methodology and instance}
\label{ssec:naming}

This paper contributes both a \emph{methodology} for building automatically-verifiable benchmarks of generative geometric reasoning and a \emph{first instance} of that methodology.
We use \textbf{\GeoGen} for the methodology --- two coupled ideas, formalised in \cref{ssec:methodology}: (i) procedural co-generation of prompts and property-list rubrics from shared source code, and (ii) a render-then-symbolic-verify pipeline driven by a declarative predicate language --- and \textbf{\GeoGenBench} for the 801-prompt plane-geometry benchmark in which we instantiate it.
The distinction matters: future \GeoGen-family benchmarks for coordinate-plane algebra, 3D solid geometry, transformation-group constructions, and engineering or architectural drawings would inherit the methodology and the predicate-language design (\cref{app:property_reference}) while extending the per-domain symbolic primitives (\cref{ssec:future_methodology}).

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The \GeoGen methodology and reusable verification toolkit.}
We formalise a two-pillar recipe for generative-geometric-reasoning benchmarks: (a) \emph{procedural co-generation}, in which a small Python generator emits the natural-language prompt and the machine-checkable rubric from the same source code, eliminating drift between the question and its grading criteria and providing a built-in contamination defence (reviewers can re-roll the engine with new seeds); and (b) \emph{render-then-symbolic-verify}, in which the model's text-emitted output is compiled to symbolic objects against which a versioned, declarative predicate language (\cref{ssec:properties}, \cref{app:property_reference}) is decided in milliseconds.
We release both pillars as reusable code (template engine + verifier + 17-predicate language v1 + a soundness audit, \cref{sec:hardening}, that closes 4.6\% of silently-skipped pilot checks across three hardening passes).
\item \textbf{The \GeoGenBench instance: 801 prompts, six frontier models, and a leaderboard with three deployment-relevant findings.}
\GeoGenBench is the methodology's first instantiation: 600 procedurally co-generated prompts (released as \texttt{bench\_generated.yaml}) plus 201 LLM-extracted curriculum prompts for breadth (released as \texttt{bench\_curriculum.yaml}).
We benchmark six frontier models from three vendors (\opus, \sonnet, \haiku, \gpt, \geminipro, \geminiflash) under three generation strategies (raw TikZ; structured intermediate-representation JSON with check-and-revise; recipe-DSL with constraint solving), reporting --- at the \cref{sec:hardening}-hardened verifier --- a strict-pass spread of \textbf{60\pp} (36.7\% to 96.7\%) across non-saturating combos, a \textbf{$\mathbf{19\times}$ cost-with-equal-pass spread} between \haiku + \rawcode (87\% at \dollars{0.017}/scen) and \opus + \rawcode (80\% at \dollars{0.329}/scen), and a vendor-specific deployment-portability finding (\cref{ssec:gemini_finding}): \geminiflash's schema-state cap blocks structured generation entirely.
The dataset, the template engine, the verification toolkit, the leaderboard data, and a Docker-packaged TikZ$\to$SVG renderer are all released under permissive licenses (\cref{app:reproduction}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Headline empirical results}

Three findings from the post-hardening pilot, in order of importance:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Strategy effect is real but smaller than the verifier's first reading suggested.}
The \structured strategy lifts every model by \textbf{3--20\pp} of strict pass rate over direct TikZ (\rawcode):
\gpt (73\% $\to$ 93\%, $+20$\pp), \sonnet (80\% $\to$ 94\%, $+14$\pp), \opus (80\% $\to$ 100\% on $N=12$, $+20$\pp), \haiku (87\% $\to$ 90\%, $+3$\pp).
The pre-hardening verifier reported gaps of 10--30\pp; the \cref{sec:hardening} audit attributes about half of that gap to silent skips of \texttt{\textbackslash pgfmathsetmacro}-style coordinates that \rawcode outputs use more often than IR-compiled outputs.
We report both numbers in \cref{sec:results} and \cref{app:property_reference}.
\item \textbf{Cost-equivalence: scaffolding beats scale.}
\haiku with \rawcode (87\% strict pass, \dollars{0.017}/scenario) \emph{dominates} \opus with \rawcode (80\% strict pass, \dollars{0.329}/scenario) on both axes --- $7$\pp more correct diagrams at $19\times$ lower cost.
Adding \structured on \haiku (\dollars{0.032}/scenario) buys a further $3$\pp at $1.9\times$ the \haiku cost.
The Pareto frontier is built almost entirely from cheap-model configurations; expensive-model \rawcode is universally dominated regardless of strategy.
\item \textbf{The recipe-DSL strategy is unstable across models.}
\sonnet uses the recipe DSL well (97\% strict, $+17$\pp over hardened \rawcode).
\gpt and \haiku do not (63\% and 73\%, both \emph{worse} than their \rawcode by $10$--$13$\pp).
A small custom DSL with built-in geometric priors transfers poorly across model families and is not a recommendable deployment strategy below the frontier-model tier.
\end{itemize}
